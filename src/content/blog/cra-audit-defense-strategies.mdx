A CRA transfer pricing audit is a serious matter. It typically begins with a notice of assessment or information request and can escalate into a full examination of your group's intercompany pricing practices. Over twenty years advising Canadian multinationals, we've seen which defense strategies succeed and which fail. The difference often lies not in the complexity of the business but in the rigor of preparation and the timing of engagement.

## Understanding the CRA Audit Process

The CRA's transfer pricing audit typically proceeds in stages. First comes the selection phase, where the CRA identifies entities for examination based on audit risk metrics—high cross-border payments, significant intangible asset transfers, or inconsistent reporting relative to comparable companies.

Next is the information gathering phase. The CRA issues information requests seeking functional descriptions, transfer pricing documentation, comparable company data, and the historical record of how transfer prices were set. These requests are detailed and demanding. Responses must be complete and accurate; inconsistencies or gaps are immediately flagged.

The examination phase follows, where CRA auditors (often with specialized transfer pricing expertise) analyze your functional analysis, economic methodology, and comparable data. They may engage external economic consultants to review technical positions. This phase typically lasts 6-18 months depending on complexity.

Finally, the reassessment phase occurs when the CRA proposes adjustments. At this point, you enter the dispute resolution process—initially through the Notice of Objection procedure and potentially through Canadian courts.

## Strategy 1: Anticipatory Documentation Excellence

The most effective defense begins before the CRA ever knocks on your door. Documentation that would withstand audit scrutiny must exist from the outset. This means contemporaneous analysis, not retroactive justification.

Consider a technology company that licenses software to related parties globally. The documentation should show that before pricing the license, the company identified comparable software licensing agreements, analyzed the software's functionality and market position, researched licensing rates paid for comparable technology, and documented the economic reasoning for the chosen royalty rate. This analysis should exist on file as of the year-end being audited—not prepared later in response to CRA requests.

We recently defended a client in a CRA audit involving intellectual property transferred to a subsidiary during restructuring. The critical factor in prevailing was contemporaneous fairness opinion documentation prepared by an independent valuation firm at the time of transfer. This documentation showed the transfer price reflected fair market value at that moment. The CRA's challenge relied on hindsight—the IP subsequently increased in value significantly—but contemporaneous documentation established arm's length pricing at the relevant time. The position was upheld.

The lesson: documentation quality determines audit outcomes more than any other factor. Invest in thorough, contemporaneous documentation before any CRA notice arrives.

## Strategy 2: Economic Analysis Depth and Rigor

The second critical strategy involves the depth and rigor of your economic analysis. The CRA increasingly employs specialized economists to review comparable company data and methodology selection. Documentation that appears rigorous on surface inspection but contains flawed economic reasoning will not survive expert scrutiny.

Effective economic analysis requires understanding your comparables intimately. Using a database that shows Company X is comparable to your enterprise is insufficient. You must understand Company X's business model, functional scope, geographic presence, and whether the comparison is truly appropriate. If your business develops intellectual property internally while Company X manufactures commodity products under contract, they're not comparable regardless of what a database algorithm suggests.

We defended a transfer pricing position involving management fees charged by a US parent to a Canadian subsidiary. The CRA challenged the fee as excessive. Our defense relied on identifying truly comparable management service arrangements from multiple sources, adjusting for differences in scope and jurisdiction, and demonstrating the fee fell within the acceptable range. The strength of the comparable company analysis—not the assertion that the fee was reasonable—carried the day.

This requires engaging economists or transfer pricing specialists experienced in forensic analysis. They understand what makes comparables valid and can articulate reasoning that survives CRA scrutiny.

## Strategy 3: Contemporaneous Transaction Records and Contemporaneity

The timing of when you establish your transfer pricing analysis matters profoundly. The CRA views documentation prepared months after fiscal year-end with inherent skepticism. How could you know what information informed your decision if you're only analyzing it now?

Contemporaneous records address this directly. Maintain records showing that transfer pricing decisions were made during the planning phase, supported by available information at that time. Email correspondence discussing pricing decisions, committee meeting minutes addressing transfer pricing, or internally prepared benchmark studies all demonstrate contemporaneous analysis.

We once represented a client facing CRA adjustment to a significant intercompany service arrangement. The difference was decisive: one version of their documentation was prepared retroactively during audit preparation, while another document in the file showed contemporaneous analysis from the relevant year. The contemporaneous document was dated and showed the economic reasoning used at the time pricing was set. The CRA position weakened substantially once contemporaneous support existed.

The practical implication: ensure transfer pricing governance occurs during the fiscal year, and document that governance. If your transfer pricing committee meets in January to discuss the upcoming year's pricing, document that meeting. If you research comparables before pricing decisions, maintain those research files. This creates an evidentiary record supporting contemporaneity.

## Strategy 4: Proactive Engagement with CRA

Many multinational enterprises treat CRA transfer pricing inquiries defensively, responding narrowly to what's requested and revealing nothing additional. This approach typically backfires. A more effective strategy involves proactive engagement that demonstrates good faith compliance and understanding.

When a CRA information request arrives, provide complete, well-organized responses that anticipate likely follow-up questions. If the CRA requests functional analyses, provide them in clear narrative form with explanatory context. If asked for comparable company data, provide comprehensively organized databases with documentation of search criteria and selection rationale.

This approach accomplishes several objectives. First, it prevents the CRA from finding inconsistencies or contradictions in your responses. Second, it demonstrates sophistication and serious compliance intent. Third, it creates an opportunity for early dialogue that might identify issues before the CRA forms entrenched positions.

We advised a client to proactively reach out to the CRA during an audit to clarify the business context of their transfer pricing. While the client's advisor made this presentation, it provided opportunity for the CRA to understand the company's actual functions and operations. Subsequent CRA positions reflected this understanding. The proactive engagement materially improved the outcome.

## Strategy 5: Competent Authority and Mutual Agreement Procedures

If CRA adjusts transfer prices and the foreign jurisdiction also adjusts the related transaction, you face economic double taxation. Canadian transfer pricing law provides relief through competent authority procedures (CAP), available under applicable tax treaties.

Under competent authority procedures, the Canadian CRA and the foreign revenue authority can mutually agree to adjust both jurisdictions' positions to eliminate double taxation. This requires demonstrating that the original transfer price (or the CRA's proposed adjustment) does not reflect arm's length pricing.

Competent authority relief has become increasingly valuable as CRA audits increase. Filing a competent authority request after the CRA assesses allows the Canadian CRA and foreign revenue authority to collaborate toward mutual agreement. This often results in better outcomes than unilateral CRA positions.

We've successfully pursued competent authority relief for clients facing double taxation from US and Canadian transfer pricing disagreements. The process requires careful documentation of the original position, the CRA's adjustment, and economic evidence supporting arm's length pricing. The Canadian CRA's competent authority function has become more accessible and effective in recent years.

## When Transfer Pricing Gets Challenged

The fundamental reality of transfer pricing audits is that positions face challenge when documentation is weak, analysis is shallow, or economic reasoning is flawed. The five strategies outlined here—excellent documentation, rigorous economic analysis, contemporaneous records, proactive engagement, and understanding competent authority relief—address the core vulnerabilities that trigger adverse audit outcomes.

Businesses facing CRA transfer pricing audits should engage specialists early, not after positions have already been challenged. Early engagement allows for assessment of defensibility and development of optimal response strategies. The cost of specialist involvement is typically a fraction of what's at stake in transfer pricing disputes.
